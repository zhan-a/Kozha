Avatar and rendering

Every animation you see on Bridgn is drawn by CWASA, a signing-avatar engine built by the Virtual Humans Group at the University of East Anglia. CWASA is the only reason the rest of the pipeline has anywhere to send its output — without it, Bridgn would be text-only.

CWASA — Character animation system

CWASA (the CWA Signing Avatar system) is a WebGL-based signing avatar engine that renders SiGML input as a 3D animation in the browser. Maintained by the Virtual Humans Group (VHG) at the University of East Anglia, CWASA is the public successor to the JASigning and Animgen avatars developed under the eSIGN and DictaSign research programmes.

Notation system

HamNoSys is the intermediate representation between parsed text and a rendered sign. Every sign in the Bridgn corpus is stored as HamNoSys, then serialised into SiGML for CWASA. The notation itself — its glyphs, the phonological categories it describes, and the reference documentation — comes from the Institute of German Sign Language at the University of Hamburg.

HamNoSys — Hamburg Notation System for sign languages

HamNoSys is a phonetic transcription system for sign languages, covering handshape, orientation, location, movement, and non-manual features. The current reference is HamNoSys 4.0 (Hanke et al., 2018). Bridgn uses HamNoSys as the canonical internal representation for every sign regardless of language; the authoring flow on the contribute page writes HamNoSys, and the translator reads it.

Sign-language databases

The signs Bridgn can render come from thirteen sources across twelve sign languages. Every source is listed with its institution, license (where declared), entry count as loaded by the translator, and the languages it contributes to. Where the upstream repository does not declare a license, we say so plainly and mark the source until the author confirms permissions.

DictaSign Corpus BSL

The European DictaSign project produced a comparable parallel corpus across four sign languages (BSL, DGS, GSL, LSF). Bridgn's British Sign Language data is drawn directly from the DictaSign BSL portion, maintained at the Institute of German Sign Language. The same corpus is aliased to the ASL translator entry, since no dedicated ASL corpus is available in this repository yet — ASL output still uses ASL fingerspelling, but lexical signs fall through to the BSL dataset.

Translation layer

Input text arrives in one of many source languages; Bridgn routes it through translation and NLP before it ever touches a sign database. Two libraries do almost all of this work.

argostranslate — offline text translation

argostranslate is the text-translation layer between Bridgn's supported input languages and the sign language's base language. It runs server-side with no network calls and packages per-pair translation models downloaded at install time. Supported pairs today: English, French, German, Spanish, Polish, Dutch, Greek, Russian, Arabic.

Maintainer
Argos Open Technologies
License
MIT License (library). Translation models are distributed per the argosopentech-published terms per pair — consult the argostranslate model registry for the authoritative statement for a given pair.
Links
argos-translate on GitHub Argos Open Technologies
Used for
All server-side text-to-text translation when the input language differs from the sign language's base language.

spaCy — NLP pipeline

spaCy is the parser, tokenizer, lemmatizer, and POS tagger that turns input text into a sequence of linguistic tokens before the sign database is consulted. Bridgn loads dedicated spaCy models for seven languages (English, German, French, Spanish, Polish, Dutch, Greek) and caches up to four concurrent models in an LRU.

Maintainer
Explosion AI
License
MIT License (library). Language models are distributed under per-model terms; the small-news and core-web models Bridgn uses are MIT-licensed as of spaCy 3.8.
Links
spaCy home spaCy on GitHub spaCy model registry
Used for
Tokenisation, lemmatisation, POS tagging, and sentence segmentation in the planner.

Deaf advisory board

The Deaf advisory board is the project's accountability body. It approves reviewer appointments, arbitrates disputes, and can remove a published sign without further discussion.

Deaf advisory board is being seated. Current status: zero candidates confirmed. Until the board is seated, no signs from community contributions are exported to the public translator — see the governance page for the full review policy.

Funding

Full transparency on where operating costs come from.

No external funding to date. Bridgn's infrastructure costs (hosting, domain, translation-model storage) are covered by the core team out of pocket. If and when external funding arrives, it will be listed here with the funder, amount bracket, and any conditions attached.

Contributor compensation

How Deaf reviewers and advisory-board members are compensated for their time.

A compensation policy is not yet in place. Drafting the policy — covering hourly rates for reviewer work, flat stipends for advisory-board meetings, and the process for requesting payment — is a prerequisite for seating the Deaf advisory board. Until that is done, no reviewers are being asked to work.

The policy will be published on the governance page as soon as it is drafted, and linked from here. We prefer to say "not yet in place" rather than describe an aspirational arrangement that hasn't been funded.
